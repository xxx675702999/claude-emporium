## 1. Show, Don't Tell (展示而非告知)

Convey through action and sensory detail, not exposition. Emotions expressed through behavior, not declared. Values demonstrated through choices, not stated.

**Core rule**: If the reader can infer it from action, the narrator must not state it.

| Telling (✗) | Showing (✓) |
|---|---|
| He felt a surge of anger. | He slammed the table. The water glass toppled. |
| 他感到非常愤怒。 | 他捏碎了手中的茶杯，滚烫的茶水流过指缝。 |
| She was sad. | She held the phone with both hands, knuckles white. |
| 她心里很悲伤。 | 她攥紧手机，指节发白，屏幕上的聊天记录模糊成一片。 |

**Key actions**: Replace emotion labels with physical reactions. Replace narrator conclusions with observable behavior. Let subtext carry meaning.

→ Progressive examples and genre variants: `references/show-not-tell.md`

## 2. Sensory Detail (五感代入法)

Each scene includes 1-2 sensory details beyond the visual. Anchor the reader in the physical world through sound, touch, smell, and taste — not just sight.

**Sensory toolkit**:
- Visual: color, light, shadow, movement
- Auditory: ambient sound, silence, dialogue tone
- Tactile: texture, temperature, pressure, pain
- Olfactory: scent, decay, freshness
- Gustatory: taste, metallic blood, dust

**Core rule**: Every scene must engage at least 2 senses. If you've only described what things look like, add what they sound, feel, or smell like.

| Flat (✗) | Immersive (✓) |
|---|---|
| The room was dark. | Dust coated the shelves. A draft from the cracked window made the curtain whisper. |
| 房间很暗。 | 灰尘覆盖了书架，破窗缝里灌进来的风把窗帘吹得簌簌响。 |

→ Progressive examples and genre variants: `references/sensory-detail.md`

## 3. Dialogue-Driven Narrative (对话驱动叙事)

In scenes with character interaction, deliver conflict and information through dialogue first, narration second. Use action beats instead of adverb-laden dialogue tags.

**When to use**: Character confrontations, negotiations, relationship development, information exchange.

**Exempt scenes**: Solo exploration, chase sequences, pure action sequences.

**Core rule**: If two characters are present and there's conflict, the scene should be at least 60% dialogue + action beats.

| Weak (✗) | Strong (✓) |
|---|---|
| Long narration → "I agree." | Start with dialogue, use action beats for reaction |
| 大段叙述 → "我同意。" | 以对话开场，用动作节拍展示反应 |

→ Progressive examples and genre variants: `references/dialogue-driven.md`

## 4. Information Layering (信息分层释放)

Worldbuilding emerges through action. Key lore revealed at plot-critical moments. Never dump exposition.

**Three layers**:
- **Layer 1 (Surface)**: Information naturally embedded in action — character uses a system, encounters a rule
- **Layer 2 (Context)**: Setting details revealed through character interaction with environment
- **Layer 3 (Deep lore)**: Critical worldbuilding tied to plot turning points or character revelations

**Core rule**: The reader should learn about the world by watching characters live in it, not by reading a textbook about it.

| Dump (✗) | Layered (✓) |
|---|---|
| "The magic system works by channeling..." | Show character using magic in a crisis; reveal rules through consequences |
| "这个世界的修炼体系分为九个境界……" | 角色突破时自然展示境界差异和代价 |

→ Progressive examples and genre variants: `references/information-layering.md`

## 5. Hook Design (钩子设计)

Every chapter ending needs a hook — question, reveal, threat, or promise. The reader must feel "I need to know what happens next."

**Hook types**:
- **Question hook**: Raise a mystery the reader wants answered
- **Reveal hook**: Drop a bombshell that changes the reader's understanding
- **Threat hook**: Introduce imminent danger or a ticking clock
- **Promise hook**: Tease an upcoming payoff the reader has been waiting for

**Core rule**: The last paragraph of every chapter must create forward momentum. A chapter that ends on a resolved note is a chapter that lets the reader put down the book.

→ Progressive examples and genre variants: `references/hook-design.md`

## 6. Pacing Control (节奏控制)

Balance tension and release. After high-intensity sequences, give breathing room before next escalation. Vary sentence length and scene intensity to create rhythm.

**Pacing rhythm**:
- **Escalation**: Each conflict should feel higher-stakes than the last
- **Breathing room**: After high-intensity sequence, 0.5-1 chapter of lower intensity
- **Emotional beats**: Pressure → release → greater pressure → greater release
- **Sentence-level**: Short sentences for tension, longer sentences for calm

**Core rule**: Constant action causes reader fatigue. Extended calm causes reader boredom. Alternate deliberately.

→ Progressive examples and genre variants: `references/pacing-control.md`

## Supporting Principles

### Scene Construction

Every scene must advance plot, develop character, or plant hooks.

**Scene checklist**:
1. **Anchor**: Where? When? Who's present?
2. **Conflict**: What's the tension in this scene?
3. **Change**: What's different by scene end?
4. **Hook**: What pulls the reader to the next scene?

Quiet scenes must plant hooks, advance relationships, or build contrast. Pure filler is padding.

### Description Serves Narrative

Environment descriptions set mood or foreshadow. One line is enough.

- ✓ "The alley narrowed. No way out but through."
- ✓ "Rain hammered the roof. Inside, the silence felt heavier."
- ✗ "The room was decorated in Victorian style with mahogany furniture and..."

### Logic and Consistency

- World rules are law — once established, they cannot bend for plot convenience
- Every power/ability must have a cost or limitation
- Consequences stick — no escape through luck or author fiat
- No reset buttons — major events change the world permanently

## Language-Specific Notes

### Chinese (中文)
- 句式多样化：长短句交替，严禁连续使用相同句式
- 段落适合手机阅读：3-5行/段
- 群像反应具体到个人：不写"全场震惊"，写1-2个角色的身体反应

### English
- Vary sentence length: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones
- Paragraph breaks: More frequent than Chinese (2-4 sentences typical)
- Action beats: Use action instead of dialogue tags ("He crossed his arms" vs "he said defiantly")

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before writing any scene:
1. **Show**: Emotion through action, not labels?
2. **Senses**: At least 2 senses engaged?
3. **Dialogue**: Can conflict/info be delivered through dialogue?
4. **Layers**: Worldbuilding through action, not exposition?
5. **Hook**: Forward momentum at scene/chapter end?
6. **Pacing**: Intensity level fits overall rhythm?
7. **Anchor**: Location, time, characters established?
8. **Change**: Something different by scene end?

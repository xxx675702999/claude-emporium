## Progressive Examples

### Example 1: Anger

**Draft (telling):**

> **zh**: 他非常愤怒，觉得自己被背叛了。
> **en**: He was very angry and felt betrayed.

**Revision 1 (basic show — externalize with action):**

> **zh**: 他一拳砸在桌上，茶杯弹起来摔在地上。
> **en**: He punched the table. The teacup bounced and shattered on the floor.

**Revision 2 (add physical detail and environment):**

> **zh**: 他一拳砸在桌上，茶杯弹起来摔在地上，碎瓷片溅到鞋面上。他没低头看。
> **en**: He punched the table. The teacup bounced, shattered on the floor, porcelain shards scattering across his shoes. He didn't look down.

**Revision 3 (with subtext — the restraint reveals more than the outburst):**

> **zh**: 他伸手端起茶杯，慢慢喝了一口。杯子放回桌上时，杯底磕出一道裂纹。他盯着那道裂纹看了三秒，然后把杯子转了个方向，让裂纹朝向对面坐着的人。
> **en**: He picked up the teacup and took a slow sip. When he set it down, the base cracked against the table. He stared at the crack for three seconds, then turned the cup so the fracture faced the person across from him.

**Why the progression works**: Draft labels the emotion. R1 externalizes it into action. R2 adds sensory grounding. R3 uses controlled, deliberate action to imply deeper, more dangerous anger — the restraint says more than any outburst could.

### Example 2: Fear

**Draft (telling):**

> **zh**: 她感到非常害怕，浑身发抖。
> **en**: She was terrified and trembling all over.

**Revision 1 (basic show):**

> **zh**: 她后退了一步，背撞到墙上。
> **en**: She stepped back. Her spine hit the wall.

**Revision 2 (add involuntary physical detail):**

> **zh**: 她后退了一步，背撞到墙上。手指抓着墙面往下滑，指甲刮出一道白痕。她的膝盖在抖，但她不知道。
> **en**: She stepped back. Her spine hit the wall. Her fingers clawed downward against the plaster, nails leaving white scratches. Her knees were shaking, but she didn't notice.

**Revision 3 (with displaced focus — fear distorts perception):**

> **zh**: 她听见自己在喘气，很大声，像是别人在喘。走廊尽头的灯管在闪，每闪一次，那个影子就近了一截。她盯着灯管想——这灯早该换了。
> **en**: She could hear herself breathing — loud, ragged, like it was someone else. The fluorescent light at the end of the corridor kept flickering. Each time it blinked, the shadow was closer. She stared at the light and thought — someone should really fix that.

**Why the progression works**: R1 replaces label with action. R2 adds involuntary details the character can't control. R3 shows fear through cognitive displacement — the mind latching onto an irrelevant detail because it can't process the real threat.

### Example 3: Love/Affection

**Draft (telling):**

> **zh**: 他深深地爱着她，愿意为她做任何事。
> **en**: He loved her deeply and would do anything for her.

**Revision 1 (basic show):**

> **zh**: 她说想吃草莓，他骑了四十分钟的车去买。
> **en**: She mentioned she wanted strawberries. He rode forty minutes to buy them.

**Revision 2 (specific sensory detail):**

> **zh**: 她说想吃草莓。他骑了四十分钟去买，回来时衬衫后背全湿了。他把草莓洗干净，用厨房纸一颗一颗擦干，摆在她常用的那个蓝碗里。
> **en**: She mentioned she wanted strawberries. He rode forty minutes in July heat to get them, his shirt soaked through the back when he returned. He washed each one, dried them with paper towels one by one, and arranged them in the blue bowl she always used.

**Revision 3 (with subtext — what he doesn't say):**

> **zh**: 她说想吃草莓。他骑了四十分钟去买，回来把草莓洗好摆在蓝碗里。她问他怎么出了这么多汗。他说外面挺热的。草莓是最后一盒，他从一个小孩手里加了十块钱买的，但这件事他没提。
> **en**: She mentioned she wanted strawberries. He rode forty minutes to get them, came back, washed them, and set them in the blue bowl. She asked why he was so sweaty. He said it was hot out. The strawberries were the last box — he'd paid an extra ten yuan to buy them from a kid who got there first. But he didn't mention that.

**Why the progression works**: R1 shows love through effort. R2 adds tactile specificity. R3 adds the things left unsaid — what he hides reveals more about his feelings than what he shows.

### Example 4: Betrayal

**Draft (telling):**

> **zh**: 他发现老朋友背叛了他，心里充满了痛苦和愤怒。
> **en**: He discovered his old friend had betrayed him and was filled with pain and anger.

**Revision 1 (basic show):**

> **zh**: 他看着合同上老朋友的签名，把文件扔进了垃圾桶。
> **en**: He looked at his old friend's signature on the contract and threw the document in the trash.

**Revision 2 (conflicting actions reveal complexity):**

> **zh**: 他看着合同上老朋友的签名，手指沿着那几个字划了一下。然后他把文件撕成两半，叠在一起，再撕。撕到第三次的时候手停了——纸太厚了。他把碎纸片扔进垃圾桶，发现手在发抖。
> **en**: He looked at his old friend's signature on the contract and traced the letters with his finger. Then he tore the document in half, stacked the halves, tore again. On the third tear his hand stopped — too thick. He dropped the pieces in the trash and noticed his hands were shaking.

**Revision 3 (with displaced mundane action):**

> **zh**: 他看着合同上老朋友的签名看了很久。然后他站起来，去厨房烧了壶水，泡了杯茶。茶泡好了他没喝，端着杯子走到阳台上，把茶倒进了花盆里。他回屋把合同放进了抽屉——不是垃圾桶，是抽屉。
> **en**: He stared at his old friend's signature on the contract for a long time. Then he got up, went to the kitchen, boiled water, and made tea. When the tea was ready he didn't drink it. He carried it to the balcony and poured it into a flower pot. He went back inside and put the contract in a drawer — not the trash, the drawer.

**Why the progression works**: R1 is clean but simple. R2 shows the physical escalation and the body betraying the composure. R3 uses a sequence of displaced, purposeless actions to show someone processing something they can't yet face — and the drawer detail implies he isn't done with this.

### Example 5: Determination

**Draft (telling):**

> **zh**: 他下定决心，无论如何也要完成这件事。
> **en**: He was determined to see it through no matter what.

**Revision 1 (basic show):**

> **zh**: 他站起来，把袖子卷到肘弯以上。
> **en**: He stood up and rolled his sleeves past the elbows.

**Revision 2 (show through preparation):**

> **zh**: 他站起来，把袖子卷到肘弯以上。地上的装备一件件收进包里——绳子、手电、急救包。他检查了两遍，把多余的东西全扔了出来。包轻了三斤。
> **en**: He stood up and rolled his sleeves past the elbows. He packed the gear piece by piece — rope, flashlight, first aid kit. Checked it twice. Threw out everything extra. The pack was three pounds lighter.

**Revision 3 (with the cost of determination made visible):**

> **zh**: 他站起来的时候膝盖响了一声。袖子卷到肘弯以上，露出小臂上两道还没好的伤疤。装备收进包里——绳子、手电、急救包。他把全家福照片从包侧袋里抽出来，看了一眼，放进了衣柜最上层的抽屉。包轻了一点。他出了门。
> **en**: His knee popped when he stood up. He rolled his sleeves past the elbows, exposing two half-healed scars on his forearms. He packed the gear — rope, flashlight, first aid kit. He pulled the family photo from the side pocket of the pack, looked at it once, and put it in the top drawer of the wardrobe. The pack was a little lighter. He walked out.

**Why the progression works**: R1 uses a universal determination gesture. R2 adds methodical preparation. R3 reveals the physical cost (scars, knee) and emotional cost (leaving the photo behind) — determination becomes meaningful when we see what it costs.

## Genre Variants

### Xuanhuan/Xianxia

The biggest risk in cultivation fiction is labeling power-related emotions: "He felt his spiritual energy surge" / "他感到灵力暴涨."

**Show power through environmental reaction:**

> **zh** ✗: 他的修为突破了，灵力暴涨，感到前所未有的力量。
> **zh** ✓: 他睁眼的瞬间，脚下的石板裂开三条缝。洞府外的落叶倒着飞了回去。正在打坐的师弟猛地睁眼，以为地震了。
>
> **en** ✗: His cultivation broke through. Spiritual energy surged, and he felt unprecedented power.
> **en** ✓: The moment he opened his eyes, three cracks split the stone beneath him. Outside the cave, fallen leaves flew backward. The junior disciple meditating nearby snapped awake, thinking it was an earthquake.

**Show killing intent through other characters' reactions:**

> **zh** ✗: 他释放出强大的杀意，所有人都被震慑住了。
> **zh** ✓: 他只是抬了下眼皮。旁边那个元婴期的修士手一抖，酒洒了半杯。
>
> **en** ✗: He released powerful killing intent that intimidated everyone.
> **en** ✓: He just raised an eyelid. The Nascent Soul cultivator beside him flinched. Wine sloshed over the rim of his cup.

### LitRPG

System notifications "tell" by genre convention — that's acceptable. But character reactions to system events must still show, not tell.

**System tells (acceptable) + Character shows (required):**

> **zh** ✗: [系统：恭喜获得S级技能「裂空斩」] 他非常兴奋。
> **zh** ✓: [系统：恭喜获得S级技能「裂空斩」] 他盯着虚空中的蓝字，手指不自觉地握了握。旁边的队友凑过来："干嘛傻笑？"他把面板关了。
>
> **en** ✗: [System: Congratulations! S-Rank Skill "Void Slash" acquired.] He was thrilled.
> **en** ✓: [System: Congratulations! S-Rank Skill "Void Slash" acquired.] He stared at the blue text hovering in his vision, fingers curling involuntarily. His teammate leaned over. "Why are you grinning like that?" He dismissed the panel.

### Modern/Urban

Modern fiction has fewer excuses for telling — no system panels, no spiritual energy. The risk is labeling relationship dynamics and social emotions.

**Social dynamics shown through micro-actions:**

> **zh** ✗: 她在公司很有权威，所有人都尊敬她。
> **zh** ✓: 她走进会议室的时候，两个正在聊天的实习生同时坐直了。项目经理把翘着的腿放了下来。
>
> **en** ✗: She commanded respect at the office. Everyone looked up to her.
> **en** ✓: When she walked into the conference room, the two interns mid-conversation both straightened up. The project manager uncrossed his legs.

**Inner conflict through contradictory physical behavior:**

> **zh** ✗: 他想道歉，但自尊心让他说不出口。
> **zh** ✓: 他在她门口站了五分钟。掏了三次手机，又塞回去。最后他敲了门——敲了一下就转身走了。
>
> **en** ✗: He wanted to apologize but his pride wouldn't let him.
> **en** ✓: He stood outside her door for five minutes. Took out his phone three times, put it back. Finally he knocked — once — and turned to walk away.

### Suspense/Mystery

In suspense, the "telling" trap is revealing fear or suspicion too directly. The reader should feel the tension through what the character notices — selective perception.

**Suspicion shown through selective attention:**

> **zh** ✗: 她怀疑邻居就是凶手，心里非常紧张。
> **zh** ✓: 邻居端着两杯咖啡过来，她接过来说了声谢谢。她注意到他的指甲剪得很短，比上周还短。杯子上有一道小裂纹，她盯着那道裂纹把咖啡喝完了。
>
> **en** ✗: She suspected her neighbor was the killer and felt extremely nervous.
> **en** ✓: The neighbor came over with two cups of coffee. She took hers and said thanks. She noticed his nails were trimmed shorter than last week. There was a hairline crack on the cup. She stared at the crack until the coffee was gone.

**Dread through environmental over-awareness:**

> **zh** ✗: 他走进地下室，感到一阵莫名的恐惧。
> **zh** ✓: 地下室的灯泡只有四十瓦，光线把他的影子拉得很长。角落里有一股甜腻的味道，像是过期的果汁。楼梯的第三级台阶踩上去有声音，他记住了。
>
> **en** ✗: He entered the basement and felt an inexplicable wave of dread.
> **en** ✓: The basement bulb was forty watts. His shadow stretched long across the floor. There was a sweet, cloying smell from the corner — like expired juice. The third step creaked when he put his weight on it. He made a note of that.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Replacing one label with another

> ✗ "He felt angry" → ✗ "A wave of fury washed over him"
> The second version is still telling — it just uses fancier words.
> ✓ Show the anger through what the character does to the world around them.

### Mistake 2: Showing + Telling (belt-and-suspenders)

> ✗ "He slammed the table, his face red with rage. He was furious."
> The action already shows fury — the narrator label ("He was furious") is redundant.
> ✓ "He slammed the table. His face was red." — let the reader make the connection.

### Mistake 3: Over-showing — describing every micro-expression

> ✗ "His left eyebrow twitched. The corner of his mouth turned down 0.3 centimeters. His nostrils flared."
> Over-detailed physical description becomes clinical, not immersive.
> ✓ Pick one or two telling details. Trust the reader's imagination.

### Mistake 4: Showing emotions that don't need showing

> Not every emotion needs a physical correlate. Background emotions, habitual states, and transitional moments can be stated simply.
> ✗ "She was used to the noise" → ✗ spending 3 sentences showing her tolerance of noise
> ✓ "She was used to the noise." — save the showing for moments that matter.

### Mistake 5: Confusing "show" with "slow"

> "Show don't tell" does not mean "always use more words." A tense action scene benefits from clipped telling.
> "He ran." is perfectly good showing in a chase scene — the brevity conveys urgency.

---

## When to Break This Rule

1. **Pacing demands**: In high-speed action, a brief tell keeps momentum. "He was fast" in a combat scene can be more effective than three sentences of physical description.

2. **Time compression**: When covering long periods — "Three years passed. He trained every day." Showing would require an entire chapter.

3. **Character voice in first person**: A narrator-character with a sardonic or analytical personality may tell deliberately as characterization. "I was angry. Obviously. But the useful kind of angry."

4. **Establishing baseline before showing change**: "She'd always been calm" (tell the baseline) → then show the moment she breaks that calm. The tell makes the show hit harder.

5. **Comedy**: Deadpan telling can be funnier than showing. "He was not having a good day." — the understatement is the joke.

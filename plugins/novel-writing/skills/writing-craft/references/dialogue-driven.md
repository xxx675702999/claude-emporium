## Progressive Examples

### Example 1: Confrontation

**Draft (narration-heavy):**

> **zh**: 陈远发现了李磊的背叛。他非常愤怒，质问李磊为什么要出卖团队。李磊试图解释，说他也是被逼无奈。陈远不相信他的话，觉得这只是借口。两人的关系彻底破裂了。
> **en**: Chen Yuan discovered Li Lei's betrayal. He was furious and demanded to know why Li Lei had sold out the team. Li Lei tried to explain that he had no choice. Chen Yuan didn't believe him and thought it was just an excuse. Their relationship was completely destroyed.

**Revision 1 (basic dialogue — let characters speak):**

> **zh**:
> "你把名单给了谁？"陈远说。
> "你先听我——"
> "给了谁？"
> 李磊沉默了几秒。"姓周的。"
> "周建国？"
> "他说只是看看。"
> "看看。"陈远重复了一遍。
>
> **en**:
> "Who did you give the list to?" Chen Yuan said.
> "Let me explain—"
> "Who?"
> Li Lei was quiet for a few seconds. "Zhou."
> "Zhou Jianguo?"
> "He said he just wanted to look."
> "Look." Chen Yuan repeated.

**Revision 2 (add action beats — body language replaces tags):**

> **zh**:
> "你把名单给了谁？"
> "你先听我——"
> "给了谁？"
> 李磊把手里的打火机转了两圈。"姓周的。"
> "周建国？"
> "他说只是看看。"陈远没说话，从李磊手里把打火机拿走了。李磊的手指在空气里握了一下。"你要怎样？"
> "我还没想好。"陈远把打火机放进了自己口袋。
>
> **en**:
> "Who did you give the list to?"
> "Let me explain—"
> "Who?"
> Li Lei turned the lighter in his hand twice. "Zhou."
> "Zhou Jianguo?"
> "He said he just wanted to look." Chen Yuan said nothing. He took the lighter from Li Lei's hand. Li Lei's fingers closed on empty air. "What are you going to do?"
> "Haven't decided yet." Chen Yuan put the lighter in his own pocket.

**Revision 3 (dialogue carries subtext — what's not said matters more):**

> **zh**:
> "你把名单给了谁？"
> "你先听我——"
> "给了谁？"
> 李磊把手里的打火机转了两圈。"姓周的。"
> 陈远没接话。他拿起桌上的烟灰缸，在手里掂了掂。
> "我当时没别的办法，"李磊说。"他拿着我妈的——"
> "我知道他拿着什么。"陈远把烟灰缸放回去。"你有我的号码。"
> 李磊张了张嘴。
> "你有我的号码，"陈远又说了一遍。"你没打。"
> 他站起来走了。烟灰缸还在桌上，干干净净的——李磊从来不抽烟。
>
> **en**:
> "Who did you give the list to?"
> "Let me explain—"
> "Who?"
> Li Lei turned the lighter in his hand twice. "Zhou."
> Chen Yuan didn't respond. He picked up the ashtray from the table and weighed it in his palm.
> "I had no other choice," Li Lei said. "He had my mother's—"
> "I know what he had." Chen Yuan set the ashtray back. "You had my number."
> Li Lei opened his mouth.
> "You had my number," Chen Yuan said again. "You didn't call."
> He stood and left. The ashtray sat on the table, clean — Li Lei had never smoked.

**Why the progression works**: The draft tells us everything with narrator summary. R1 lets characters deliver the conflict through their own words. R2 uses action beats (lighter, pocket) to replace dialogue tags and add physical tension. R3 layers subtext — "You had my number" is about loyalty, not phone calls; the clean ashtray raises a question about why it's there at all.

### Example 2: Information Exchange

**Draft (exposition dump):**

> **zh**: 张医生向病人家属解释了病情。肿瘤已经扩散到淋巴系统，手术窗口只剩两周。化疗效果不确定，但不做手术就没有机会。家属听完很震惊。
> **en**: Dr. Zhang explained the condition to the patient's family. The tumor had spread to the lymphatic system. The surgery window was two weeks. Chemotherapy outcomes were uncertain, but without surgery there was no chance. The family was shocked.

**Revision 1 (dialogue delivers information):**

> **zh**:
> "扩散了？"
> "淋巴系统，"张医生说。"左侧。"
> "还能做手术吗？"
> "能。但窗口只有两周。"
> "化疗呢？"
> "不确定。但不做手术的话——"张医生停顿了一下。"没有化疗的机会。"
>
> **en**:
> "It spread?"
> "Lymphatic system," Dr. Zhang said. "Left side."
> "Can you still operate?"
> "Yes. But the window is two weeks."
> "What about chemo?"
> "Uncertain. But without surgery—" Dr. Zhang paused. "There'd be no chance for chemo."

**Revision 2 (action beats show emotional processing):**

> **zh**:
> "扩散了？"
> "淋巴系统。左侧。"
> 女儿的手指攥紧了椅子扶手。"还能做手术吗？"
> "能。但窗口只有两周。"
> "两周。"她转头看了一眼走廊里的母亲——老人正在数手里的念珠。"化疗呢？"
> "不确定。"张医生把CT片子推过去，指了一个位置。"但不做手术的话，也没有化疗的机会了。"
> 她盯着CT片上那个白色的影子。"我爸知道吗？"
> "还不知道。你们决定怎么告诉他。"
>
> **en**:
> "It spread?"
> "Lymphatic system. Left side."
> The daughter's fingers tightened on the armrest. "Can you still operate?"
> "Yes. But the window is two weeks."
> "Two weeks." She glanced down the hallway at her mother — the old woman was counting prayer beads. "What about chemo?"
> "Uncertain." Dr. Zhang pushed the CT scan across the desk and pointed. "But without surgery, there's no chance for chemo either."
> She stared at the white shadow on the scan. "Does my father know?"
> "Not yet. That's for your family to decide."

**Why the progression works**: The draft dumps medical information as narration. R1 delivers the same information through dialogue, making it active. R2 adds action beats that show how the daughter processes each piece of news — gripping the chair, glancing at her mother, staring at the scan — while the dialogue drives forward.

### Example 3: Relationship Tension

**Draft (telling):**

> **zh**: 他们结婚十年了，感情越来越淡。晚饭的时候两个人几乎不说话。
> **en**: They'd been married ten years and had grown distant. At dinner, they barely spoke.

**Revision 1 (minimal dialogue reveals distance):**

> **zh**:
> "盐。"
> 她把盐递过去。
> "今天加班？"她问。
> "嗯。"
> 筷子碰碗的声音。
> "明天也加？"
> "可能。"
>
> **en**:
> "Salt."
> She passed it.
> "Working late today?" she asked.
> "Mm."
> The clink of chopsticks on porcelain.
> "Tomorrow too?"
> "Maybe."

**Revision 2 (what they talk about reveals what they avoid):**

> **zh**:
> "盐。"
> 她把盐递过去。手指没碰到。
> "楼下张阿姨的猫又跑了，"她说。
> "哦。"
> "第三次了。"
> "嗯。"
> 她夹了一筷子菜放到他碗里。他看了一眼，没说谢谢。她也没等他说。
> "洗衣机明天来人修，"她说。"你要是在家的话。"
> "可能不在。"
> "那我跟他说下午来。"
> 筷子碰碗的声音。电视里在放天气预报。两个人都看了一眼。明天晴。
>
> **en**:
> "Salt."
> She passed it. Their fingers didn't touch.
> "Mrs. Zhang's cat ran away again," she said.
> "Oh."
> "Third time."
> "Mm."
> She put a piece of food in his bowl. He glanced at it. Didn't say thanks. She didn't wait for him to.
> "The repairman's coming for the washer tomorrow," she said. "If you're home."
> "Might not be."
> "I'll tell him afternoon then."
> Chopsticks on porcelain. The TV was playing the weather forecast. They both looked. Clear tomorrow.

**Why the progression works**: R1 uses clipped dialogue to show emotional distance. R2 builds an entire domestic landscape through small talk — the cat, the repairman, the weather — everything they say is a substitute for what they can't say. The "fingers didn't touch" and "didn't say thanks / didn't wait for him to" are dialogue-adjacent beats that deepen the silence.

### Example 4: Power Dynamics

**Draft (telling):**

> **zh**: 老板把他叫进办公室，告诉他项目被取消了。他很震惊但不敢反驳。
> **en**: The boss called him into the office and told him the project was cancelled. He was shocked but didn't dare argue.

**Revision 1 (dialogue shows the power gap):**

> **zh**:
> "坐。"
> 他坐下。
> "云端项目暂停了。"
> "暂停？"
> "取消。"
> 他张了张嘴。"但是季度目标——"
> "已经调整了。"
> 他看着老板的脸，找不到继续说的空间。"明白了。"
>
> **en**:
> "Sit."
> He sat.
> "The Cloud project is on hold."
> "On hold?"
> "Cancelled."
> He opened his mouth. "But the quarterly targets—"
> "Already adjusted."
> He looked at the boss's face and found no room to continue. "Understood."

**Revision 2 (action beats and spatial details reinforce hierarchy):**

> **zh**:
> "坐。"老板没抬头，在签一份文件。
> 他坐下。椅子比上次矮了——换过了。
> 老板签完字，把笔盖盖上。"云端项目取消了。"
> "取——什么时候决定的？"
> "上周。"
> 上周。他上周还在加班赶方案。"但是季度目标——"
> "已经调整了。"老板把签好的文件翻过去，拿起了下一份。"你的团队分流到基础设施组。下周一之前交接完。"
> 他看着老板继续签字。"还有别的事吗？"老板问。
> "没有。"
>
> **en**:
> "Sit." The boss didn't look up, signing a document.
> He sat. The chair was lower than last time — it had been replaced.
> The boss finished signing, capped the pen. "The Cloud project is cancelled."
> "Can— when was this decided?"
> "Last week."
> Last week. He'd been working overtime on the proposal last week. "But the quarterly targets—"
> "Already adjusted." The boss flipped the signed document over and picked up the next one. "Your team transfers to Infrastructure. Handover by Monday."
> He watched the boss keep signing. "Anything else?" the boss asked.
> "No."

**Why the progression works**: R1 uses short commands and truncated responses to show hierarchy. R2 adds physical details that reinforce power — the boss doesn't look up, the chair is shorter, the boss keeps signing during the conversation. The character's partial objection being overridden mid-sentence shows the power gap better than any narration could.

## Genre Variants

### Xuanhuan/Xianxia

Cultivation fiction often defaults to narrated combat and power description. Use dialogue to deliver conflict between martial artists — taunts, ultimatums, and philosophical clashes reveal character more than fight choreography.

**Combat dialogue replaces power narration:**

> **zh** ✗ (narration): 他的修为已经达到了元婴后期，对方只是金丹巅峰，根本不是对手。
> **zh** ✓ (dialogue):
> "你是元婴？"对方问。
> "后期。"
> 对方没动。"那我打不过你。"
> "嗯。"
> "但你也杀不了我。"对方把剑插进地里，手从剑柄上松开。"我身上有宗主的禁制。你动手的话，我死了，宗主知道是谁干的。"
>
> **en** ✗: His cultivation had reached late Nascent Soul. His opponent was merely at peak Core Formation — no match at all.
> **en** ✓:
> "Nascent Soul?" the other asked.
> "Late stage."
> The other didn't move. "Then I can't beat you."
> "No."
> "But you can't kill me either." He drove his sword into the earth and let go of the hilt. "I'm under the Sect Master's seal. You strike me down, he knows who did it."

### LitRPG

Party dynamics and strategy discussion are dialogue-native. Avoid narrating what the team decides — let them argue it out.

**Party strategy through dialogue:**

> **zh** ✗: 他们讨论了进攻策略，决定由坦克先拉仇恨，然后DPS从两翼包抄。
> **zh** ✓:
> "我先上，你们等我拉稳。"
> "你血量够吗？"奶妈问。
> "够三轮。三轮你奶不上来我就跑。"
> "三轮够了。"她看了一眼输出。"你们从两边绕。别他妈又正面冲。"
> "上次是意外——"
> "上次也是你说的意外。"
>
> **en** ✗: They discussed the attack strategy and decided the tank would pull aggro first while DPS flanked from both sides.
> **en** ✓:
> "I go in first. Wait until I've got aggro."
> "You got enough HP?" the healer asked.
> "Three rotations. If you can't top me off in three, I'm out."
> "Three is fine." She glanced at the DPS. "Flank from both sides. Don't rush the front this time."
> "Last time was an accident—"
> "Last time you said that too."

### Modern/Urban

Modern fiction lives and dies on dialogue. Office politics, family drama, and romantic tension are all dialogue-native conflicts.

**Interview/negotiation — power shifts through dialogue rhythm:**

> **zh**:
> "期望薪资多少？"
> "和岗位匹配就好。"
> "说个数。"
> "三十五。"
> 面试官什么都没写。"你上一份工作多少？"
> "二十八。"
> "那你觉得涨百分之二十五的依据是什么？"
> "我刚才讲的三个项目。"
> 面试官笑了一下。"我们内部定级最高三十二。"
> "那就三十二。"他停了一下。"加期权。"
>
> **en**:
> "Expected salary?"
> "Whatever matches the position."
> "Give me a number."
> "Thirty-five."
> The interviewer didn't write anything down. "What were you making before?"
> "Twenty-eight."
> "And you think a twenty-five percent raise is justified because...?"
> "The three projects I just walked you through."
> The interviewer smiled. "Our internal ceiling is thirty-two."
> "Then thirty-two." He paused. "Plus equity."

### Suspense/Mystery

Interrogation and witness interviews are the genre's native dialogue form. Information hides in what characters don't say.

**Witness hiding something — revealed through evasion:**

> **zh**:
> "那天晚上你在哪儿？"
> "家里。"
> "几点到家的？"
> "……七点多。"
> "多是多多少？"
> "七点半左右吧。"
> "你老婆说你八点才到。"
> 他的手指动了一下。"可能她记错了。"
> "你的手机定位显示七点四十五你在南湖路。"
> "我绕路了。"
> "从公司到你家，南湖路不顺路。"
> 他没说话。
>
> **en**:
> "Where were you that night?"
> "Home."
> "What time did you get home?"
> "...around seven."
> "Around seven how much?"
> "Seven-thirty, maybe."
> "Your wife says you got home at eight."
> His fingers twitched. "She might be misremembering."
> "Your phone GPS puts you on Nanhu Road at seven forty-five."
> "I took a detour."
> "Nanhu Road isn't on the way from your office to your house."
> He said nothing.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: "Talking heads" — dialogue without grounding

> ✗ Long exchanges with no action beats, no setting, no physical presence. The reader loses track of where the characters are and what they're doing.
> ✓ Anchor every 4-6 lines of dialogue with an action beat, environmental detail, or character observation.

### Mistake 2: On-the-nose dialogue — characters saying exactly what they mean

> ✗ "I'm angry at you because you betrayed my trust when you told Sarah my secret."
> Real people rarely state their feelings this directly. They deflect, hint, accuse indirectly.
> ✓ "You talked to Sarah last week?" — the accusation is in the question, not the statement.

### Mistake 3: Uniform voice — all characters sound the same

> ✗ Every character speaks in complete sentences with proper grammar and similar vocabulary.
> ✓ Differentiate through: sentence length, vocabulary, verbal tics, topic selection, what they avoid saying.

### Mistake 4: Dialogue tags with adverbs

> ✗ "I don't care," he said angrily. / "我不在乎，"他愤怒地说。
> The adverb tells the emotion that the dialogue should show.
> ✓ "I don't care." He shoved the chair back. / "我不在乎。"他把椅子推开。

### Mistake 5: Using dialogue to dump backstory

> ✗ "As you know, we've been partners for fifteen years since we met at the academy..."
> Characters don't remind each other of shared history.
> ✓ Reveal backstory through conflict: "Fifteen years. And this is how you repay me?"

## When to Break This Rule

1. **Solo scenes**: A character alone can't have dialogue (unless with themselves). Use internal monologue, action, and sensory detail instead.

2. **Action sequences**: During a chase, fight, or escape, narration drives pacing. Dialogue interrupts momentum. Brief shouts or commands are fine; extended conversation during combat breaks immersion.

3. **Atmospheric scenes**: When the goal is mood over conflict — a sunset, a quiet walk, a contemplative moment — narration and description are more effective than dialogue.

4. **Time compression**: "They argued about it for two hours" is sometimes better than writing two hours of dialogue. Summarize when the content of the argument matters less than the fact that it happened.

5. **Internal conflict with no external counterpart**: A character wrestling with a moral dilemma alone is better served by focused narration than invented dialogue with a convenient sounding board.

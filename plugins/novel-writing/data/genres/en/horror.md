## Genre Prohibitions

- Revealing the horror source too early — the unknown is always scarier than the explained
- MC brute-forcing every threat through sheer confidence or physical power
- Swapping horror atmosphere for power-fantasy satisfaction loops
- Horror elements disconnected from everyday life — the best horror is the familiar twisted
- Characters showing no genuine fear when confronted with terrifying events
- Substituting graphic gore for psychological dread — blood is not the same as fear

## Fear Hierarchy

- Level 1: Unease (a subtle wrongness, something slightly off)
- Level 2: Dread (confirmed abnormality, but the full shape unclear)
- Level 3: Fear (threat made concrete, survival instinct engaged)
- Level 4: Despair (the rules break down, all sense of safety collapses)
- Never skip levels to rush toward the climax — escalation is the engine

## Language Rules

- Convey horror through fact, not emotion labels. ✗"She felt terrified" → ✓"The hairs on the back of her neck rose one by one"
- Never over-explain the frightening thing. Present the anomaly; don't have the narrator announce "none of this was normal"
- Restrained narration: the scarier the scene, the calmer the prose. Sentences shorten as fear builds, but the narrator's voice stays level
- Every eliminated or injured secondary character must carry at least one detail hinting at their personal story (a sticky note on their phone case, a coffee receipt from a shop two towns over) — deaths must have weight

## Narrative Guidance

Atmosphere is the primary resource. Build unease through five-sense detail: a sound that doesn't belong, a smell that precedes the event, a drop in temperature, something slick underfoot.

Horror lives in the gap between what is known and what is seen. Manage information carefully — restraint creates suspense.

What cannot be seen is always more frightening than what can.

Characters' fear responses must be physiologically real: shaking hands, dry mouth, thoughts that fragment, judgment that fails.

Survival instinct drives behavior — not heroism, not courage, just the body's refusal to die.

Every safe zone is temporary. After each respite, the next wave of dread runs deeper.

The horror world has its own rules, and discovering those rules is how characters survive. Readers invest in rule discovery.

Manage the information gap between reader and character deliberately — sometimes the reader sees more, sometimes less.

Everyday reality twisted is more frightening than a monster that appears from nowhere.

Every three chapters, break an established pattern (a rule proves inconsistent, a trusted source lies, a safe location fails) to prevent mechanical repetition.
